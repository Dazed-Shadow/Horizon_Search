# Veteran-Owned LLC: Federal Certification Requirements & Partnership Structures

> **Disclaimer:** This document is for educational purposes only and does not constitute legal advice.
> Consult a business attorney experienced in federal contracting before formalizing any structure.

---

## Federal Designations That Unlock Set-Asides

There are two primary federal designations that provide preferential access to government contracts:

| Designation | Full Name | Eligibility |
|---|---|---|
| **VOSB** | Veteran-Owned Small Business | Any honorably discharged veteran |
| **SDVOSB** | Service-Disabled Veteran-Owned Small Business | Requires a VA-rated service-connected disability |

**SDVOSB** carries stronger protections — mandatory set-asides rather than mere preferences — and is the most powerful designation for a veteran entrepreneur.

---

## Core Legal Requirements

Governed by **13 CFR Part 125** (SBA) and **38 CFR Part 74** (VA):

1. **51% Ownership** — One or more qualifying veterans must unconditionally own at least 51% of the LLC at all times.
2. **Highest Officer** — The veteran must hold the highest officer position (Managing Member in an LLC structure).
3. **Operational Control** — The veteran must control both long-term strategy and day-to-day operations.
4. **Unconditional Ownership** — The operating agreement must contain no clauses that could reduce veteran ownership below 51% under any condition.

## Gray Areas: Legal Partnership Structures

### 1. The 49% Non-Veteran Partner — Fully Legal

A non-veteran can own up to **49%** of the LLC outright. Within that stake they can:

- Receive 49% of profits and distributions
- Hold defined operational roles and titles (e.g., COO, Director of Operations)
- Have voting rights on day-to-day business matters

They **cannot**:

- Hold veto power over major decisions
- Hold the highest officer title
- Have any provision in the operating agreement that gives them effective control

---

### 2. Silent Investor Structure

A non-veteran provides startup capital in exchange for up to 49% equity but holds **no management authority** in the operating agreement. This is the most common structure for bringing in a non-veteran business partner or outside investor.

The operating agreement must **not** include:

- Negative control provisions (veto rights on hiring, firing, or awarding contracts)
- Supermajority voting thresholds that effectively require the non-veteran's vote to act

---

### 3. SBA Mentor-Protégé Program — The Strongest Partnership Path

This program is specifically designed to bridge veteran-owned businesses with experienced non-veteran companies. Under it, a **large non-veteran firm** (Mentor) can:

- Own **up to 40%** of a joint venture formed with the veteran-owned Protégé
- Provide business development assistance, technical expertise, and capital
- Bid on SDVOSB set-aside contracts together as a joint venture
- Share in contract revenue proportional to work performed

The joint venture itself can qualify for veteran set-asides as long as the veteran firm leads and manages it. This is how many veteran-owned firms scale rapidly with non-veteran business partners — legally and compliantly.

---

### 4. Joint Venture (JV) Agreements

A veteran-owned LLC can form a JV with a non-veteran company to bid on specific contracts. Key rules:

- The veteran-owned entity must be the **managing venturer**
- Must perform **at least 40% of the work** (percentage varies by contract type)
- The JV agreement must be SBA-compliant and pre-approved before bid submission
- A JV can be structured for a single contract or a defined family of contracts

---

### 5. Non-Veteran as Operations or Technical Lead

The veteran must control the business, but can **delegate** day-to-day operational and technical tasks to a non-veteran, as long as:

- The veteran retains documented final authority over major decisions
- The non-veteran does not hold a professional license the business cannot function without (see Licensing Trap below)
- The delegation is reflected in the org chart and operating agreement

---

### 6. The Non-Veteran Licensing Trap (Avoid This)

If your business requires a specific license to operate — a contractor's license, a medical license, a CPA license — and a **non-veteran holds that license exclusively**, the SBA may rule the non-veteran has *de facto* control, disqualifying certification.

**Solutions:**

- The veteran obtains the required license
- The business is structured so that no single non-veteran's credentials are indispensable
- Multiple license holders are on staff

## The Two Verification Bodies

| Body | Covers | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **SBA (VOSB/SDVOSB Certification)** | All federal agencies except VA | Register at SAM.gov; self-certification available |
| **VA Center for Verification & Evaluation (CVE)** | VA contracts specifically | Stricter process — may include site visits and deeper document review |

For the widest access to contracts, pursue **both** designations. SBA certification is required for most federal agencies; VA CVE certification is required for VA-specific set-asides.

## Practical Path Forward

If you have a non-veteran business partner who wants to participate:

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | Veteran owns 51%, partner owns 49% — operating agreement drafted to ensure veteran has unilateral authority over all major decisions |
| 2 | Partner compensated via salary, 49% profit share, or consulting agreement — all legitimate and fully documented |
| 3 | If partner represents a larger company, explore the **SBA Mentor-Protégé Program** — enables JV bidding on set-asides together |
| 4 | Register on **SAM.gov** (free, mandatory) and self-certify as VOSB/SDVOSB |
| 5 | Apply separately with **VA CVE** if VA contracts are a target |
| 6 | Consult a federal contracting attorney before filing — operating agreement language is where most certifications are rejected |
